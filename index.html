Crucial Talks on Interim Government: Quaid-e-Azam Meets Congress President

"If the Viceroy accepts an Interim Government based solely on Nehru's terms, I cannot accept this position." — Quaid-e-Azam

Quaid-Nehru Meeting Lasts Over an Hour

New Delhi: In a dramatic turn of events, a meeting took place today between the political giants of the subcontinent. Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah and Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru met at the residence of the latter to discuss the volatile political situation.

The meeting, which lasted for approximately 80 minutes, has drawn the attention of the entire world press. Sources suggest that the conversation focused heavily on the Viceroy's recent proposals regarding the formation of an Interim Government.

Unrest in Palestine: Underground Networks Active

Jerusalem: Reports emerging from the Middle East indicate a sharp rise in underground activities. British intelligence has uncovered several hidden caches of equipment intended for insurgent use in the holy land.

Authorities have tightened security around key government buildings following threats from militant factions. The mandate government has issued strict curfews in sensitive zones to prevent further escalation of violence.

Meanwhile, international delegates are scrambling to find a diplomatic resolution. The Arab Higher Committee has expressed grave concerns over the continuous influx of immigrants, warning of severe consequences.

Back on the ground in Jerusalem, the diplomatic deadlock is manifesting as tangible fear. Barbed wire zones—known locally as "Bevingrads"—are expanding. Shops in the Old City are shuttering early, and the uneasy coexistence in mixed neighborhoods is rapidly disintegrating into suspicion.

Key Points of Discussion:

  • Immediate cessation of hostilities.
  • Review of immigration quotas.
  • Security for religious sites.
  • Role of international observers.

MESSAGE TO MUSLIMS

The Grand Mufti has issued a call for unity in these testing times. Steadfastness is required.

Russia Vetoes Draft Treaty Proposals

Paris: The peace conference has hit another snag as the Soviet delegation exercised its veto power on the draft treaty concerning Italy. The move has surprised Western diplomats who were expecting a consensus.

Russian Foreign Minister Molotov argued that the clauses regarding reparations were unfair and economically crippling. He demanded an immediate revision of the territorial adjustments proposed in the Adriatic region.

The United States and Great Britain have called for an emergency session to resolve the deadlock. Observers fear that this disagreement could delay the final peace settlement for months.
